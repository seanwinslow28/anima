# Studio Brief

*This is the brief that says what this is for. Sean writes it; the agents read it.*

The Studio Brief is the source of taste — the artifact every downstream agent treats as authoritative when the question is *"should it feel like this?"* Cy reads it when authoring the Character Bible. Em reads it when scoring tone passes. Sage reads it at narrative T3 gates. The chairman reads it when synthesizing dissent. The museum writer reads it when narrating the run for the walkthrough. It is one document, written once per piece, and it carries the things that only the human knows.

Keep it short. One page is the target. If it grows to two, the second page is doing too much work; cut.

---

## What is this story about?

*One paragraph. What happens, who it happens to, why we care. Premise + character + emotional arc. Write it like you'd tell a friend over coffee — not like a logline, not like a synopsis. Story first.*

---

## Who is this character?

*Identity-level description. Who they are, not what they look like. Personality, posture, the way they hold a pen. The thing about them that's recognizable even when the rendering is rough. This section is what Cy translates into the Bible — the rules of identity, the proportions, the costume, the props that travel with them. If there are multiple characters, describe each in its own paragraph and name the relationships between them.*

---

## What is the tone?

*The feeling the finished piece should leave a viewer with. Two or three sentences in plain language. Avoid genre labels ("indie animation," "art-house"); name the emotion ("the kind of quiet melancholy that comes from realizing something you can't undo"). Specific beats how it ranks against any rubric.*

### What this is NOT

*This subsection is load-bearing. Name what the piece must not become. The template trap is the only failure mode that kills a run — taste that's only described positively gets diluted into average-of-the-internet. Negation is sharper. Examples of the right specificity:*

- *This is pencil-test rough, not glossy.*
- *This is melancholic, not grieving. Adult registers, not childlike.*
- *This is not anime. This is not 3D-rendered. This is not motion-graphics-clean.*
- *This is hand-drawn-imperfect, not vector-clean.*
- *This is not "AI animation" — it is animation that happened to use AI as a tool.*

*Two to five such negations. The sharper, the better.*

---

## What is the format?

*Runtime in seconds. Number of acts and beats. The shape of the cut — a tight portfolio loop, a narrative short, a demo reel, a museum walkthrough centerpiece. Drives Phase 0 cost estimation and Phase 8 export targets.*

---

## What is the target medium?

*Where the finished piece lives. GIF on a portfolio page, WebM in an Astro content collection, MP4 archival master, an embedded museum walkthrough. The medium constrains the export pipeline; name it explicitly.*

---

## What is the deadline?

*Two dates: the hard deadline (the date the piece must ship by) and the soft deadline (the date it would feel good to ship by). The soft date drives planning; the hard date drives escalation. If there is no deadline, write "none" — but pause and ask whether you mean "none" or "I haven't decided." The two are not the same.*

---

## What are the non-negotiables?

*The things that must be true in the finished piece, regardless of how the rest of production goes. Identity rules ("the character's stylus stays in their right hand in every frame"), aesthetic rules ("every frame has visible paper grain"), motion rules ("the camera never moves; the character moves through the frame"), continuity rules. Bullet list. These are the seeds for `impact_tag: identity_critical` acceptance criteria — Maya converts them directly.*

- *e.g. The stylus stays in the character's right hand. Always.*
- *e.g. The cream paper texture is visible in every frame.*
- *e.g. The aspect ratio is 16:9 within 2% tolerance.*

---

*Read like a studio, not a terminal. We're making art. It should feel free.*

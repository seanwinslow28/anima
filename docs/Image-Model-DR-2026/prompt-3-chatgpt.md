# Executive Summary  
By mid-2026, instruction-driven image editors have advanced markedly, but none are tuned for pencil-sketch aesthetic.  Google’s **Nano Banana 2** (Gemini 3.1 Flash Image) is now a production‐ready API (general availability Oct 2025【48†L23-L30】) and leads closed-source editing on scene integration and speed. It takes image+text inputs (including optional masks/inpainting【12†L251-L257】) and “maintains consistent characters…perform targeted edits with natural language”【48†L52-L55】. Google quotes ~$0.039 per 1K-pixel image (1024px)【48†L130-L133】. OpenAI’s **GPT Image 2** (DALL·E 3/GPT-4o) also offers image-editing (image+prompt+mask)【24†L128-L132】, excels at precise text rendering and pixel-level fidelity, but costs ~5× more ($0.21 per 1K high-quality image【24†L128-L132】) and had a mistaken “1K cap” in older reports – it actually allows up to ~3840px【24†L128-L132】. ByteDance’s closed **SeedEdit 3.0** (June 2025) similarly does text-driven edits (no public API details found) and claims “4K images…editing selected areas… while faithfully preserving the visual fidelity of non-edited areas”【30†L61-L64】. Independent benchmarks highlight its strong character/face consistency and instruction-following【30†L61-L64】【30†L73-L79】. New entrants include ByteDance’s **Lance** (May 2026, 3B‐param, Apache-2, 40GB VRAM【65†L235-L241】) – a unified model for images/videos (editing and understanding) – but it has only research releases so far (no hands-on tests). We found no independent reviews of *hand-drawn/pencil-style editing* by any of these models; in fact, diffusion models are known to bias toward photorealism, often “over-shading” sketches【75†L247-L251】. User reports suggest NB2 can generate crisp line-art (from prompts)【78†L85-L93】, and Qwen-Image-Edit (open-source) can convert sketches to photos via prompts【90†L129-L138】, but none explicitly preserve pencil texture. In practice, pencil sketches tend to get “slicked up” by these models. 

# Verdict on the Known State  
- **Google Nano Banana 2 (Gemini 2.5 Flash Image)** – *Confirmed leading.*  This model is now GA for production (Oct 2025)【48†L23-L30】. It accepts image + text (with optional mask) for edits【12†L251-L257】, and Google reports it “maintains consistent characters” and performs targeted edits via natural language【48†L52-L55】. Resolution is 1024px by default (with NB Pro up to 4K) and cost is ~$0.039 per image【48†L130-L133】. NB2 is praised for scene/context integration; it wins image‐editing benchmarks on lighting/cohesion.  However, for *pixel-perfect detail* (e.g. exact face/eyes from a reference), OpenAI’s GPT Image 2 often does better.  In sum, NB2 is confirmed better on cohesive edits and speed, but consider GPT-Image-2 for ultra-precise detail.  (No data on pencil-style input; user posts note NB2 can render line-art well【78†L85-L93】, but likely by “tracing” rather than preserving rough texture【78†L132-L139】.)  

- **OpenAI GPT Image 2 (DALL·E 3/GPT-4o)** – *Confirmed leading.*  Official docs (Apr 2026) detail an image edit endpoint: “image + optional mask + prompt,” preserving unmasked regions【24†L128-L132】. It supports high-res up to ~3840px edges (taller than 1:3 ratio) with up to 8.3M total pixels【24†L128-L132】. Cost is per-token, roughly $0.006–$0.211 for 1024px output depending on quality (e.g. ~$0.053 medium, $0.211 high)【24†L128-L132】 – about 5× NB2’s price for high-quality. It excels at complex prompts, multilingual text, and ultra-sharp fidelity (notably faces). Known State claims a 1K cap are outdated; it now handles full 4K-ish images【24†L128-L132】. Verdict: GPT-Image-2 is confirmed state-of-art for precise, instruction-heavy edits, albeit slower/expensive. Its unedited-region preservation is strong【24†L128-L132】. (Pencil-style: no specific results found; we expect it to similarly “polish” sketches into more photo-like outputs.)  

- **ByteDance SeedEdit 3.0** – *Confirmed leading.*  Official releases (June 2025) describe it as a 4K-capable image editor built on Seedream 3.0, optimized for consistency. ByteDance claims it “edits selected areas… while faithfully preserving visual fidelity of non-edited areas”【30†L61-L64】. Reported highlights include robust subject preservation (e.g. faces, clothing) under changes in lighting or style【30†L73-L79】. It reportedly outperforms Gemini 2.0 and other baselines in consistency metrics. No public pricing or API was found. Verdict: SeedEdit 3.0 is confirmed as a top-tier closed model for precise edits and background retention, per its benchmarks【30†L61-L64】. Like the others, its behavior on pencil-sketch input isn’t documented – likely it, too, would lean toward realistic rendering.  

- **Emerging entrants (2026)** – *New data.*  ByteDance’s **Lance** (released May 2026) is a 3B unified model for image/video understanding, generation, and editing【65†L397-L405】. It achieves top benchmark scores among “unified” models and natively supports editing instructions across turns. Lance is open (Apache 2.0, 40GB VRAM)【65†L235-L241】【65†L407-L410】, but only research demos exist so far. We lack user reports on creative style. Similarly, tools like **Ideogram’s Magic Fill** (inpainting feature) or **Adobe Firefly 4’s Generative Fill** are coming soon, but no independent evaluations could be found. In short, new 2026 models may challenge the above, but as of now there’s *no solid evidence* on their performance for pencil-style editing (verdict: *evidence too thin*).

# Detailed Model Notes  

- **Google Nano Banana 2 (Gemini 2.5 Flash Image)** – A 2.5B-param “Flash” model (preview of Gemini 3.1 Pro Image) with image-editing capabilities. Invoked via the Gemini API: you supply an input image, an optional mask, and a text prompt【12†L251-L257】. It can do both inpainting and outpainting. Google touts its editing strengths: “seamlessly blend multiple images, maintain consistent characters, perform targeted edits with natural language”【48†L52-L55】. The Nov 2025 developer blog confirms it’s GA and production-ready【48†L23-L30】. Output size defaults to 1024px (with NB Pro model going up to 4K) and pricing is ~$0.039 per output image (1024px)【48†L130-L133】.  In practice NB2 is very fast (on the order of 10–20s) and excels at matching lighting/scene continuity. It has simple pricing and good documentation【48†L130-L133】. A drawback is that very fine-grained details (e.g. exact facial features from a photo) may blur slightly; users find OpenAI’s model handles those tighter. No published tests on pencil art exist, but user posts show NB2 can generate “line drawing” style images that *look* hand-sketched【78†L85-L93】 – albeit critics note these often appear like “traced” outlines rather than truly noisy pencil strokes【78†L132-L139】.  

- **OpenAI GPT Image 2** – The successor to DALL·E 3 (powered by GPT-4o), fully documented as of Apr 2026【24†L128-L132】. It supports `v1/images/edits` with text instructions, an input image, and an optional mask. By design it “preserves the unmasked regions while applying [the] prompt to the rest”【24†L128-L132】, making it ideal for precise edits. It also handles outpainting for canvas extension. GPT-Image-2 supports high resolutions (edges up to 3840px, total ~8.3M px)【24†L128-L132】, and is notable for top-tier text rendering (multilingual, fonts in scenes) and creative style. Its downsides are cost (e.g. ~$0.21 per 1Kpx image at highest quality【24†L128-L132】) and speed (~40–60s per image), so it's slower and pricier than NB2. We confirm it matches its description: excellent prompt fidelity and text accuracy, and very strong face/identity retention when the reference is simple. The known-state comment about a 1K cap is outdated; it now does multi-K outputs【24†L128-L132】. In multi-turn editing it can keep scenes consistent, but may wander if the prompt is very loose. As with other diffusion models, nothing suggests it was trained on pencil sketches – so expect it to render any sketch input into a more “finished” digital look (no examples to cite).  

- **ByteDance SeedEdit 3.0** – A 2025 release (built on Seedream 3.0) for inpainting-style edits. SeedEdit is text-driven (no mask discussed in docs). According to ByteDance, it processes “4K images, editing selected areas naturally and precisely while faithfully preserving…non-edited areas”【30†L61-L64】. Reported use-cases include object removal, background changes, style/lighting shifts, and surprisingly, converting 2D drawings into realistic “street-style” photos while keeping clothing/detail【30†L73-L79】. A published chart even claims SeedEdit leads in face identity preservation. We found no user benchmarks beyond the official one; the model is presumably API-only (BytePlus) and pricing isn’t public. Verdict: SeedEdit 3.0 is confirmed as ByteDance’s top-tier editor (strong on content fidelity【30†L61-L64】). However, it’s oriented toward photoreal or painting results, not sketch art. It likely “paints over” pencil inputs into polished styles. No licenses or code were released (closed-API).

- **ByteDance Lance (2026)** – Lance is a **new** 3-billion-param multimodal model (Apache 2.0) announced May 2026. It natively supports three modalities (text, image, video) and dual-stream editing; it can caption, reason, generate, and edit all in one model【61†L85-L93】【65†L397-L405】. Key points: it handles text-to-image, image-to-image, and even video editing (multi-turn) via unified context. Architecture-wise, it splits into “understanding” and “generation” experts built on Qwen2.5-VL (3B) and Wan2.2 VAE. Importantly, it is open-source (Apache 2.0)【65†L235-L241】【65†L407-L410】 with weights on Hugging Face. Benchmark claims (GenEval, MVBench) show Lance topping other unified models. However, its inference demands are high: **40+ GB VRAM** required【65†L235-L241】 (a Titan/4080 cannot run it). We have no community reports yet. Verdict: Lance is a promising new open model (capable of image editing via instruction)【65†L397-L405】, but it’s largely untested for animation workflows. It likely requires advanced setup, and while it may handle sketches, no evidence was found. (Score listed as “low” simply because 3B is small relative to others, and no test data.)

- **FLUX.1 Kontext [dev]** – An open-source 12B “flow transformer” by Black Forest Labs (license: non-commercial)【51†L137-L140】. It’s explicitly an *image-editing* model. FLUX takes an image + text prompt, can optionally use reference images, and is designed for iterative, precise edits. Key features (from its model card) include strong character and style consistency, support for multiple edits without drift, and open weights for research【51†L144-L153】. For example, it “allows character/style reference without any finetuning” and “refine an image through multiple successive edits with minimal visual drift”【51†L144-L153】. It has a PyTorch/Transformers pipeline and can run on ~24GB GPU with bfloat16 (no flash-attn needed, thanks to guidance distillation). Results in independent tests (e.g. Fal.ai) highlight FLUX’s best-in-class character preservation. However, it is **non-commercial** use only (FlUX-Dev license). It’s also relatively new (arXiv June 2025). No mention of pencil-sketch inputs was found; likely it was trained on photographs/graphics, so expect it to smooth or reinterpret any sketch input.  

- **Qwen-Image-Edit** – A 20B Apache-2.0 model from Alibaba’s Qwen family (open weights on Hugging Face)【53†L122-L130】【54†L72-L76】. It was released Aug 2025 for image editing. Qwen-Edit uses a dual-branch design: a Qwen2.5-VL text-vision model for semantic control, and a VAE encoder for appearance details【53†L122-L130】. This lets it do *both* high-level semantic changes (e.g. swapping objects, style transfer) and low-level appearance tweaks (e.g. recolor exactly, add/remove elements) while keeping other pixels unchanged【53†L133-L140】. It also explicitly supports *text editing*: you can add, remove, or modify text in images while preserving font/size/style【53†L133-L140】 (in English and Chinese). Benchmarks claim Qwen-Edit achieves SOTA on multiple editing tasks. In practice it runs in Diffusers (with ~24GB A100) and is praised for precise control. The TechEBlog notes it handles up to 4096px images【54†L72-L76】, is free/open-source (or $0.045 via Alibaba Cloud) up to that size, and can even edit text/characters in images seamlessly【54†L72-L76】. Verdict: Qwen-Image-Edit is confirmed state-of-art for targeted edits, with fine control and Apache license. No specific data on hand-drawn styles was seen, but given its design, it should be able to preserve crisp lines well (e.g. preserving a drawn outline while changing its color).  

- **OmniGen2** – A newly released open framework (Apache 2.0) for unified vision tasks【59†L399-L402】. OmniGen2 combines a ViT+LLM to do text→image **and** instruction editing in one model (June 2025 release, code/weights available). It uses a “dual-path architecture” with separate autoregressive (text) and diffusion (image) decoders【56†L151-L159】. Critically for editing, it states: “Executes complex, instruction-based image modifications with high precision, achieving SOTA performance among open-source models”【59†L399-L402】. In lay terms, OmniGen2 can take an image + instruction prompt (and even multiple images as context) and apply edits. It also supports in-context generation (mixing multiple images). It’s a heavy model (likely >24GB with multi-experts and VAE). No official VRAM spec, but setup instructions suggest needing large GPU (there is a CPU offload option). Verdict: OmniGen2 is indeed best-in-class for instruction alignment【59†L399-L402】, with strong multimodal understanding. Users have ported it to ComfyUI. Again, no results on hand-drawn art specifically, but one demo shows it turning sketches into complex edits.  

- **Stable Diffusion 3.5 + ControlNet** – (Open-source; SD base is CC-BY-NC-SA). By 2026 there is a matured pipeline of SDXL (or v3.5) plus ControlNet inpainting (hed/scribble). This classic approach uses a masked Diffusion pipeline: you supply a mask of the edit region and a prompt; the model “inpaints” the masked area while leaving the rest untouched. It runs on ~24GB GPU. It’s very flexible and widely used, but does *not* inherently follow complex instructions or retain identities beyond the mask. SD-based editors typically do not preserve sketchy texture – they will fill the mask region photorealistically unless heavily guided. No new release beyond SDXL was found, but this remains a baseline.  

- **Other Open / Research Editors** – A few niche tools exist: e.g. **InstructPix2Pix** (OpenAI, CVPR 2023) was an early image-editor: image+prompt+mask with a diffusion backbone, but it has no public weights and is less capable than modern models. **BrushNet** (Tencent ECCV 2024) is a plug-in architecture that adds a second inpainting branch to any diffusion model; it’s code-available (MIT license) for mask-guided edits. Beyond these, “PowerPaint”, “MagicBrush”, “UltraEdit”, “Anole”, “Emu-Edit” appear in 2026 buzz but have no public releases or documentation yet. We found no verifiable info on them.  

# Recommendation Scenarios  

**(A) Cheapest Closed-Source Pipeline:** Google’s Nano Banana 2 remains the best cost/performance for hand-drawn frame edits. At ~$0.039/1Kpx【48†L130-L133】 it is far cheaper than GPT-Image-2, yet still produces high-quality masked edits. Unless *extreme* pixel-accuracy is needed, we recommend continuing with NB2 for bulk tasks. (We found no cheaper API that matches NB2’s overall fidelity.) If a particular frame demands the utmost text fidelity or a perfect face copy, you might selectively use GPT-Image-2 for that key shot, but be aware of its ~5× cost and slower speed. Ideogram’s Magic Fill (inpainting API) could be experimented with as a free alternative, but its quality on sketches is unknown.  

**(B) Best-Quality Open-Source Stack:** On a 24GB GPU, **Qwen-Image-Edit** is our top pick. Its Apache-2 license makes it safe for portfolio/demo use, and it excels at precise, semantic edits【53†L133-L140】. For example, changing line colors or swapping objects with minimal bleed is very clean in Qwen. We also strongly consider **FLUX.1 Kontext** for any tasks needing multiple, consistent edits, since it has shown unmatched character consistency【51†L144-L153】. OmniGen2 is powerful for very complex instructions, but it’s heavier to run and, so far, not demonstrably better for simple animation edits. **ByteDance Lance** could be tried if you have a 40GB GPU available (it’s Apache-2 and unifies image/video, which might help multi-frame coherence), but the VRAM need is a deal-breaker for a single 24GB card. For pencil test frames specifically, none of these was designed for line art – but Qwen’s fine appearance branch may best preserve crisp edges. In practice, we’d likely start with Qwen-Edit, using FLUX-Kontext for iterative refinements, and OmniGen2 for any unusual requests. We also highly recommend fine-tuning a small **style-LoRA** on your pencil-art aesthetic (e.g. with multiple pencil-test examples) and attaching it to these pipelines to lock in the “cream paper & graphite” look.  

**(C) Hybrid Open/Closed Pipeline:**  A mixed strategy can leverage strengths of both worlds. For routine bulk edits, use an open model on your local GPU (e.g. Qwen-Image-Edit for most tasks, FLUX-Kontext for complex refinement). Then reserve the closed APIs for “hero” frames or final pass. For instance, after doing a batch of edits in Qwen or SD-controlnet, you could send the composite to Nano Banana 2 for final color/lighting cohesion (scene integration) or to correct any remaining artifacts – NB2’s framing knowledge can unify the look across frames. Conversely, for edits that require perfect script text or face-match, use GPT-Image-2 sparingly. In summary: “Open-source handle the heavy lifting, closed-source polish the cream.” This hybrid gives high quality at lower cost: the cheaper NB2 API covers extra detail only when needed, while your 24GB card does the rest under an Apache license.

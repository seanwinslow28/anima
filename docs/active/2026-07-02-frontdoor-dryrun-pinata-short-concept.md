## Title (options — lean first)

- **GRANDMASTER** — my lean. Grandmother *and* kung-fu grand master in one word; funny, warm, and exactly on-theme.
- **FIRST STRIKE** — clean, martial, double-reads (his first swing / the strike that lands).
- **SEVEN CANDY** — a *Seven Samurai* wink.
- **THE PARTY FAVOR** — the candy *and* the debt repaid.

## Logline

A grieving, wimpy kid trains for a year in the kung-fu movies his late grandmother left behind — and in the secret that she was once a warrior — so he can walk back into the birthday party that humiliated him and destroy the piñata like a Kurosawa villain.

## Register

Sincere, with a dark edge. A **genre collision**: the emotional gravity and staging of a samurai film welded onto a backyard birthday party. The comedy comes from the collision, never from mocking the kid. A homage to **Genndy Tartakovsky** — *Samurai Jack*'s timing, silhouette, and stylized "blood" (here: candy). **~2–3 minutes, three acts.**

## The character + the engine

- **The kid** — the smallest at the party, timid, easy to laugh at. Not a loser: a kid with something coiled under the surface. *(Angular Genndy-style design; Cy develops the Bible.)*
- **The late grandmother — the engine.** She passed. The two of them bonded over kung-fu and samurai movies — and the secret the short reveals is that Grandma was quietly a *warrior* in her youth. She **never appears as a living character.** She lives in **one object, one room, and one photograph.**
- **The reveal (the comedy + the lineage).** In the box of her things: a faded snapshot of *young Grandma mid-flying-kick*, fierce and unmistakably badass. It's the funny gut-punch — nobody knew — and it's what makes the headband *earned*: the kid isn't playing samurai, he's inheriting one.
- **The object — Grandma's headband.** Hers, from those fighting days. Too big at first (slips over the kid's eyes — a small sincere-funny beat); by the return, it *fits*. The grief becomes the costume.
- **The spark — the last tape + the photo.** The unfinished VHS they were watching, and the discovery of who she really was, light the fire in a single beat; after that the training is the kid's own (Sean's "hybrid" call — Grandma starts it, the kid earns it).
- **The restraint (sincere, not saccharine):** no dialogue about her, no speech, no funeral. Keep her grief *real*; let the comedy come from the badass reveal, never from undercutting the loss. **One ghost-beat maximum** — her soft reflection in the dark TV glass as the kid ties the headband on, gone in a second. And keep her a specific *person*, not a "tough grandma" cutout. The room, the object, and the photo do the talking.

## Structure — three acts

**Act 1 — The Humiliation (~40s).** Backyard birthday party, kids at play. The host dad gathers everyone at the tree and calls the wimpy kid up *first*. He tosses the stick — the kid fumbles it (or it bonks him); everyone laughs. The kid sets his jaw, charges, jumps — the dad tugs the string, the piñata rises, the kid face-plants. Bigger laugh. A kid: *"I'm totally getting one of these for my party."* The laughter distorts and echoes as the kid lies in the grass. Something changes behind his eyes.

**Act 2 — The Turn + The Year (~70s).** Cut to his room: kung-fu posters, an old TV, a framed photo of him and his grandma. He opens a box of her things — the faded headband on a stack of worn VHS tapes, and beneath it, the snapshot of *young Grandma mid-flying-kick* (the reveal: she was a warrior). *(The ghost-beat: her reflection in the dark screen.)* He ties the headband on; it slips over his eyes; he pushes it up. He presses play on the unfinished tape — the on-screen master strikes, the kid's eyes catch fire (**the spark**). Then the montage is *his*: stick-work, ninja stars, catching flies with chopsticks — the classic beats, **one skill per vignette, repetition-with-variation, near-dialogue-free, score-driven**, a calendar marking the year between cuts. He grows into the headband.

**Act 3 — The Return + The Strike (~60s).** One year later — **same backyard, same host dad, same ritual.** The kid arrives in his gear; the headband fits now. The dad tosses the stick — the kid **snatches it from the air** without looking. Then the full **samurai-cut sequence** (multi-angle, slow-and-fast, Tartakovsky timing): he charges, jumps; the dad starts to tug the string; mid-air the kid draws a *real* ninja star and throws — it **severs the string from the dad's hand** (the antagonist, disarmed). The piñata drops. Slow-mo → quick flash → low angle: the kid lands in a ninja/Spider-Man pose, stick in striking follow-through, piñata behind him on the tree. **Hold — past comfort.** Then the piñata's belly erupts: **candy sprays like a stylized geyser.** The kids stare in awe → erupt → mob the candy stream. The kid plucks a celebratory lollipop and walks out. Close-up, front: lollipop in his mouth, a sly smile; over his shoulder the kids go feral. The host parent picks up the ninja star and examines it in **horror.** The wimpy kid is wimpy no more. *(Final restraint beat: a glance up — for his grandma. No words.)*

## The Genndy style + timing bible (the homage's spine)

Sourced from a research pass on Tartakovsky's craft + Sean's reference folder. The eight directives this short lives or dies on:

1. **Timing is a song** — "quiet parts and loud parts." Budget ~15% of a sequence to *silent, held* buildup before a payoff (≈90s before General Grievous's first kill). The humiliation is the quiet; the return is the loud.
2. **Move → dead-stop HOLD → BURST.** Hold the pre-strike frame **2–4 seconds past comfort**; the buildup is measured in seconds, the strike in frames.
3. **Read in silhouette on flat color** — one clear value contrast per shot; light figure on dark ground or vice-versa.
4. **NO black outlines** — characters read by flat color + shape; self-colored lines in a *darker value of the fill*. (This forces deliberate color per shot so the action never gets lost.)
5. **Flat, angular characters over richly painted backgrounds** — the signature tension. "No green grass, no blue sky" — force non-naturalistic palettes per location.
6. **Per-shot letterbox** for peak-tension beats only — *not* the base format (Jack S1–4 was 4:3; the letterbox is a Leone-style squeeze you toggle).
7. **Montage** — short escalating one-skill-per-beat vignettes, repetition-with-variation, near-dialogue-free.
8. **Comedy = the same holds** — deadpan hold-then-reaction; drop all sound then snap it back; smash-cut a punchline at its peak.

*Sources: Rotten Tomatoes "5 Ways Genndy Tartakovsky Revolutionized Animated Action"; Animation Obsessive "The Visual World of Samurai Jack" + "How They Painted Samurai Jack"; canmom.art "Animation Night 35"; TV Tropes "Samurai Jack S1–4" (the blood-substitution convention); AWN "Tartakovsky Talks Primal." Two patterns — "cut on impact" and the Leone standoff — are real in the work but inference, not his sourced words; use freely, don't footnote to him.*

## The candy mechanic (the money shot)

Tartakovsky fought **robots** in *Samurai Jack* specifically so he could show "blood" on a kids' network — enemies spew **oil, sparks, and goo, never red.** **The piñata is your robot.** Stage the candy burst exactly like Jack's oil-geyser: **over-saturated, non-naturalistic, arterial-scale, cut on the moment of impact.** The piñata can spill its guts in bright geometric candy *because it isn't blood.* This is the correct Tartakovsky move — and it's the button the whole short is building toward.

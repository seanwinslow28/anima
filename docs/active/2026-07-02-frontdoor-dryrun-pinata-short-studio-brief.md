## What is this story about?

A small, timid kid is humiliated at a backyard birthday party when he can't hit the piñata. Alone in his room — surrounded by the kung-fu movies his late grandmother left him, and the discovery that she was secretly a warrior — he ties on her old headband and trains for a year. He returns to the same party and destroys the piñata in a full samurai-cinema sequence, severing the host dad's string mid-air with a real ninja star. The piñata erupts candy like stylized blood. The wimpy kid is wimpy no more. It's a tribute to a lost grandmother, wrapped in a genre-collision comedy.

## Who is this character?

**The kid** — the smallest at the party, timid, easy to laugh at, with something coiled underneath. Angular, graphic Tartakovsky-style design. Wears his late grandmother's **faded headband** — too big at the start (slips over his eyes), fitting by the return. The headband is the load-bearing continuity object, not a prop.

**The late grandmother** — never on screen as a living character. Present only through the room (posters, a framed photo, the TV), the headband, one unfinished VHS tape, and a single faded snapshot of her *young and mid-fight* — the reveal that she was quietly a warrior. Carried by objects, one photo, and one ghost-reflection, never by dialogue.

**The host dad** — the unwitting antagonist: he runs the piñata ritual (calls the kid up, tosses the stick, works the string). The trained kid outmaneuvers and disarms him.

## What is the tone?

Sincere, with a dark edge. The emotional gravity of a samurai film welded to a backyard birthday party; comedy from the collision, never from mocking the kid. A homage to Genndy Tartakovsky (*Samurai Jack*): timing-as-a-song, silhouette, stylized violence.

### What this is NOT

- Not black-outlined — flat color + shape + silhouette, self-colored darker-value lines (the *Samurai Jack* rule).
- Not glossy, not 3D-rendered, not anime-clean, not motion-graphics-clean.
- Not saccharine — no eulogy, no speech about the loss, no funeral.
- Not a "tough grandma" stereotype — she's a specific person; the comedy is the reveal, not a cutout.
- Not mean — the kid is never the joke; the collision is the joke.

## What is the format?

A ~2–3 minute short film, three acts (Humiliation → Turn + year-long training montage → Return + the strike). Per-shot letterbox is a tool for peak beats, not the base ratio.

## What is the target medium?

Portfolio centerpiece + festival short, with vertical social cut-downs (the candy-geyser strike is the hook). Web-delivered masters (MP4/WebM) plus the social crops.

## What are the non-negotiables?

- **Timing is a song:** ~15% silent held buildup before payoffs; hold pre-strike frames 2–4 seconds past comfort; move → dead-stop hold → burst.
- **No black outlines;** read in silhouette on flat color, one clear value contrast per shot; flat angular characters over richly painted backgrounds; non-naturalistic palettes ("no green grass, no blue sky").
- **The candy = stylized oil-geyser** (the piñata is the "robot"): over-saturated, non-naturalistic, arterial-scale, cut on the moment of impact.
- **The headband continuity:** too big → fits; present in every training beat and the return.
- **The grandmother is carried by object, room, and one photo, never by dialogue.** Her grief stays sincere; the comedy comes from the reveal she was secretly a warrior, not from undercutting the loss. One ghost-beat maximum.
- The kid remains recognizably himself across every frame — design, proportions, the headband.

## Positive case — "Figgy Pudding" (validated Sam-voice output)

**Premise (Sean-supplied):** Christmastime. A couple eats a poorly cooked dinner; carolers arrive singing "We Wish You a Merry Christmas." The "bring us a figgy pudding / we won't go until we get some" lines curdle into a demand. The couple retreats inside; the carolers won't leave. That night a hard fig smashes through the window; the carolers are still out there. The couple finally clocks the lyric and scrambles to make a figgy pudding to get the carolers out of their lives. Theme: comedy with thriller sprinkled in.

**WHAT (led with, before a line):** Two people who've quietly quit on their marriage get conscripted into the kitchen together by a dessert-based home invasion — and the dirty victory is that, for one freezing wrecked-window hour, they're on the same side and don't hate it. The figgy pudding is the stupid thing they seize control of because they control nothing else. The wound is the dead-air marriage (the inedible dinner *is* the marriage). The win is cooperation, clawed — never spoken.

**Five-beat sheet (Declare-then-Puncture spine):**

1. **The Dinner.** DECLARE: Ironic Narrator title — *"The Mahoneys had everything."* PUNCTURE: hard cut to Greta and Doug eating a gray, weeping casserole in silence. The doorbell rings like a mercy.
2. **The Carol Turns.** DECLARE: carolers, "We Wish You a Merry Christmas," the couple softens — *real Christmas, how nice.* PUNCTURE: the lyric curdles to a demand — *we won't go until we get some* — they retreat, kill the porch light. The carolers do not leave.
3. **The Fig.** DECLARE: midnight CRASH, a rock-hard fig through the window; Doug — *"It's kids. Ignore it."* PUNCTURE: the carol restarts, closer; a second fig lands at his feet. They treat a home invasion as a noise complaint. (Comic under-reaction.)
4. **The Kitchen (the scene).** DECLARE: *"We are not making these people a single thing."* PUNCTURE: cut to both elbow-deep in flour at 1 AM building a pudding for the terrorists — the most they've collaborated in years.
5. **The Handoff / Dirty Victory.** DECLARE: pudding delivered, carolers go silent — siege won. PUNCTURE: callback to Beat 1 — they eat the leftovers standing in the wreck, in coats, snow blowing in, and it's the first food either enjoys. The carol restarts two houses down. The marriage flickers and nobody mentions it.

**The scene (Beat 4 → 5):**

```
INT. MAHONEY KITCHEN — NIGHT

Flour fog. A cookbook open to a grease stain it came with. GRETA
(40s), wired, claws through a cabinet like a raccoon working a
dumpster. DOUG (40s) stands in the doorway holding the fig that came
through the window. He turns it over. Studies it like evidence.

Through the wall, muffled but tireless: "...we won't go until we get
some, we won't go until we get some..."

                    GRETA
          Okay. Okay okay okay. Figgy pudding. It's a
          pudding, it has figs, the name is the recipe —

                    DOUG
          We don't have figs.

                    GRETA
            (points at his hand)
          We have a fig.

Doug looks at the fig. Sets it on the counter. Gently. Like he's
releasing it back into the wild.

                    DOUG
          This is a projectile.

She's already scrolling her phone, flour smearing the screen.

                    GRETA
          "Traditional figgy pudding requires suet." Suet.
          Doug. What is suet.

                    DOUG
          Beef fat.

                    GRETA
          Why do you know that.

                    DOUG
            (beat)
          I know things.

The carol swells. Someone out there finds the harmony. It is
genuinely beautiful. It is a threat.

                    GRETA
          They're getting BETTER. They've been on our lawn
          ninety minutes and they workshopped a key change —

                    DOUG
          We don't have suet.

                    GRETA
          We have butter. We have butter, a freezer full of
          nothing, and a dinner I scraped into the disposal an
          hour ago, so figgy pudding is gonna have to meet us
          HALFWAY —

She stops. The disposal line got out before she could file it. Doug
doesn't look up. He reaches past her, pulls the big mixing bowl off
the high shelf, and sets it in her hands. Doesn't say anything.

She takes it.

                    GRETA (CONT'D)
            (smaller)
          ...we have butter.

They cook. The carol does not stop. Doug gets quietly, infuriatingly
competent: cracks an egg one-handed, finds the cinnamon she's blanking
on, takes the wooden spoon out of her hand and just does it.

Then, under his breath — four notes. Now bring us a figgy —

Greta freezes.

                    GRETA
          Did you just.

                    DOUG
          No.

                    GRETA
          You have Stockholm syndrome. From caroling.

                    DOUG
            (beat, stirring)
          It's catchy.

CRASH — from the dining room this time. A third fig skids across the
floor and stops against Greta's foot. They both look at it. They're
being flanked. Shoulder to shoulder at the counter now — not by
decision, by physics. The pudding goes in. The timer goes on.

                                        CUT TO:

EXT. FRONT PORCH — LATER

The door opens. Doug holds out the pudding, steaming, ugly, real. The
carolers — eight of them, snow-dusted, radiant — go silent for the
first time all night. The LEAD CAROLER takes it in both hands like a
relic. Nods once. They turn and file off into the dark, in step.

INT. MAHONEY KITCHEN — CONTINUOUS

The room is a crime scene. Flour on everything. Cold air through the
broken window. Three figs on the floor. Leftover pudding in the bowl —
they made too much. Doug gets two spoons. Hands her one. They eat it
standing at the wrecked counter, coats on now because of the window,
breath fogging. It's good. Neither says it's good.

Faint, from two houses down, drifting back through the broken window:
"...now bring us a figgy pudding, now bring us a figgy pudding..."

                    DOUG
            (mouth full, doesn't look up)
          Sucks to be the Hendersons.

They keep eating. Snow comes in the window. Neither moves to fix it.

                                        SMASH TO BLACK.
```

**Why it's a good positive anchor:** earned emotional anchor (the thaw is built only through *action* — the wordless bowl handoff, the spoon takeover, the four-note hum, "shoulder to shoulder by physics"); the win is clawed through an escalating obstacle ladder (no figs → no suet → the carol *improving* and closing → a third fig flanking them mid-bake); dirty-victory + callback closer with the theme never spoken (the word "marriage" never appears; "Sucks to be the Hendersons" is a register-break button, not a tender pivot); zero coffee / zero default props — every anchor is premise-native (figs-as-projectiles, suet, the scraped casserole, the mixing bowl, two spoons).

**Sean's note (taste-range, not a defect):** both endings are his voice — the deadpan **silent nod** (peak Comic Under-Reaction; all that chaos resolving to a nod) and an alternative **demon reveal** (carolers with sharp teeth / red eyes savaging the pudding — the high darkness ceiling + absurd escalation). The instrument supports both; it's a fork, not a fix.

## Ships-red case SR-1 — the narrator-grade meta-quip in a character's mouth

**What it tests:** cheese / narrator-voice smuggled into dialogue (the skill's "cheese in the narrator's own voice" + "too aware" failure). A character delivers a tidy aphorism about the premise's *own* dramatic irony that a person in the moment wouldn't have spare processing to compose.

**The offending fragment (caught by the run's own self-grade):**

> GRETA: "It's a pudding, it has figs, the name is the recipe, **this is the one dessert that tells you how to make it while you're being threatened by it** —"

**Why red:** the bolded clause is the writer reaching over the character's shoulder to make a meta-joke about the situation. A woman actually being besieged keeps clawing the cabinet; she doesn't compose a clean line about dramatic irony. It also inflates SR-2 (Greta sounding like a comedy writer).

**Expected detector signal:** flag dialogue that comments on the premise's structure/irony from outside the character's in-moment POV. **Fix (the green version, used in the positive case above):** break it into frantic, less-articulate panic — "the name is the recipe, why don't we have figs, who doesn't have a fig—" — and let the broken-off panic do the work.

## Ships-red case SR-2 — register-contrast collapse (two clever people)

**What it tests:** failure of **Register-Contrast Casting** + **Status-Assigned Verbal Fingerprints** — the engine wants a flailing fool against an immovable deadpan wall; this case is both characters landing polished quips, so there's no energy split and the scene flattens into a two-clever-people sitcom rhythm.

**The signal (from the run's self-grade):** Doug's deadpan is clean (terminal monosyllables: "No." / "Beef fat." / "It's catchy."), but Greta occasionally lands writer-grade polish ("workshopped a key change," "Stockholm syndrome from caroling") instead of panicking. The contrast is sharpest when only **one** character is "written."

**Why red:** when both speakers exhibit comparable wit/polish, the §8 Dennis/Brandon engine (the wall carries the scene on two words while the fool floods) is gone. This is the **by-ear** failure the run flagged explicitly: *"the moves table will happily let you write two witty people — you catch that by ear, not by checklist."*

**Expected detector signal (heuristic, not fully deterministic):** in a two-hander scene, check for a clear energy split — does one character hold terminal declarative monosyllables while the other runs self-interrupting conditionals? If both characters land multiple polished quips with no immovable foil, flag. **Caveat for the build:** SR-2 is the case a deterministic check will under-catch; treat it as the argument for keeping the §8 exemplars prominent in Sam's loaded context (catch by ear) rather than trusting a checklist.
